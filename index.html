The Audit Risk Model
AR = IR × CR × DR
RMM = IR × CR  |  Level of Assurance = 1 − AR
IR (Inherent Risk): Risk account is misstated before any internal controls. Auditor CANNOT change it — only assess it.

CR (Control Risk): Risk that client's IC will not prevent or detect a misstatement. Auditor CANNOT change it — only assess it. In pure substantive audits, CR is assumed HIGH.

DR (Detection Risk): Risk that substantive procedures will not detect a misstatement. Auditor CAN change this by doing more or less substantive testing. Inversely related to RMM — high RMM means DR must be set low.

Sampling risk (DR): Sample does not represent the population. Reduce by increasing sample size. Can never be zero.
Non-sampling risk (DR): Auditor makes a mistake during testing. Reduce through training and supervision. Can theoretically be zero.

Consider: volume, value, strength of controls, cost of testing vs. cost of substantive.

Review Engagement — Key Details
What procedures are performed: Primarily inquiry of management and key personnel, and analytical procedures. If something appears implausible, additional procedures are performed — but this is not systematic testing.

What is NOT done in a review: No tests of controls. No confirmations (unless implausibility identified). No reperformance. No inspection of source documents systematically.

The assurance statement: "Based on our review, nothing has come to our attention that causes us to believe the financial statements do not present fairly..." — this is negative assurance. You are saying you didn't find anything wrong, not that everything is right.

Independence: Required — a review is an assurance engagement. If independence is impaired, the accountant cannot issue a review report.

Going concern: The reviewer must consider whether the entity can continue as a going concern for the next 12 months. If material uncertainty exists, it must be disclosed.
Compilation Engagement — Key Details
What it is: The accountant takes information provided by management and puts it into the format of financial statements. No verification of any kind is performed.

What is NOT done in a compilation: No audit procedures. No review procedures. No assessment of whether the information is accurate. No independence requirement. No going concern assessment.

The report language: "We have not audited, reviewed, or otherwise attempted to verify the accuracy or completeness of the information provided." — zero assurance.

Notice to Reader: This is the report attached to compiled F/S. It explicitly warns users not to rely on the F/S for significant decisions without additional verification.

When it is appropriate: Small private companies with no external users requiring assurance. Internal management use. Situations where cost of a review or audit is not justified.

Licence required: Only CPA designation — no public accounting licence needed.

Common Trap: Review vs. Compilation
A reviewer who loses independence mid-engagement cannot simply downgrade to a compilation without informing the client and getting agreement — the client may have specifically required a review (e.g. for a lender).

A compilation accountant who discovers fraud or material misstatement should communicate to management and consider withdrawing — even though no assurance is given, the accountant cannot knowingly be associated with misleading F/S.

Independence is required for reviews but not compilations — this distinction is frequently tested.

What an Audit Quality Review Case Asks You to Do
You are given a description of how an audit was conducted — the procedures performed, the issues found, and how the auditor responded. You need to:

1. Identify accounting issues — are the client's accounting treatments correct under GAAP/IFRS?
2. Identify audit issues — did the auditor perform appropriate and sufficient procedures?
3. Evaluate whether the audit opinion issued was appropriate given the issues found.
4. Recommend what the auditor should have done differently.
Identifying Accounting Issues — What to Look For
—Revenue recognition: Is revenue recorded when goods are shipped / services delivered? Any bill-and-hold, channel stuffing, or early recognition?
—Estimates: Are estimates (allowance for doubtful accounts, useful lives, warranty reserves, goodwill impairment) reasonable and supported?
—Going concern: Are there indicators the entity cannot continue for 12 months? Is it properly disclosed?
—Related party transactions: Are they disclosed? At arm's length? Properly valued?
—Contingent liabilities: Should a provision be recorded? Is it disclosed if it should not be recorded?
—Asset valuation: Impairment? Write-downs? Inventory at lower of cost or NRV?
—Classification: Are items in the right accounts? Current vs. non-current? Expense vs. asset?
—Disclosure: Are all required notes present? Is anything misleading or omitted?
Identifying Audit Issues — Was the Audit Performed Well?
—Insufficient procedures: Did the auditor test the right assertions? Were sample sizes appropriate? Were strong procedures used for high-risk areas?
—Wrong procedures for the assertion: e.g. using inquiry alone (weak) for a high-risk valuation assertion instead of reperformance or confirmation.
—Timing issues: Was testing done at interim when it should have been at year end given weak controls?
—Independence issues: Did the auditor have a financial interest, personal relationship, or other threat to independence?
—Professional skepticism: Did the auditor accept management's representations without corroboration? Did they push back on estimates?
—Documentation: Are conclusions supported? Are there working papers for significant judgments?
—Materiality: Was materiality set appropriately? Were all misstatements above performance materiality addressed?
—Subsequent events: Did the auditor search for subsequent events? Were any Type I events missed?
—Opinion: Was the opinion appropriate given the misstatements and scope limitations found?

Key Quality Control Concepts
Professional skepticism: The auditor must maintain a questioning mind and critically assess audit evidence. Accepting management's word without corroboration is a quality failure.

Sufficient appropriate evidence: Sufficient = enough quantity. Appropriate = relevant and reliable. Both are required — lots of bad evidence is not enough.

Specialists: Auditors are not experts in everything. For complex valuations (goodwill, pensions, environmental liabilities) a specialist should be engaged. The auditor assesses the specialist's work but does not share responsibility — the auditor remains solely responsible for the opinion.

Management representations: Cannot substitute for other audit evidence on material matters. Written rep letters are required but are the weakest form of evidence.

Engagement Quality Control Review (EQCR): Required for public company audits — a second partner reviews the significant judgments before the report is issued.

Uncorrected misstatements: Must be evaluated both individually AND in aggregate. A string of individually immaterial misstatements can be material together.
Common Audit Quality Failures to Identify in a Case
—Auditor accepted management's estimate without independently verifying it
—Only inquiry used for a high-risk assertion — should have used confirmation or reperformance
—Sample size too small for the level of risk
—Confirmations not sent or non-responses not followed up
—Related party transactions not identified or disclosed
—Going concern not assessed when there were clear indicators
—Subsequent events procedures not performed or incomplete
—Materiality not reassessed at conclusion stage
—Misstatements not communicated to those charged with governance
—Independence threat not identified or safeguarded against
—Clean opinion issued when a scope limitation or misstatement warranted modification

Procedure Writing Format
Every procedure answer should contain three things:

1. The procedure name (Inspect, Confirm, Reperform, Inquire, Observe, Analyze)
2. What specifically you are looking at (a sample of sales invoices, the bank confirmation, the aged AR report)
3. What you are checking for / the assertion tested

Bad: "Check that revenue is recorded correctly." — no procedure name, no specific document, no assertion.

Dual-Purpose Tests
A dual-purpose test simultaneously gathers evidence for both a control test and a substantive test.

Example: Inspecting a sales invoice for the GM's approval signature tests the Authorization control (is the control operating?) AND the Existence/Validity assertion (did this sale actually occur and get approved?).

These are efficient because one procedure does two jobs — useful when budget or time is limited.
